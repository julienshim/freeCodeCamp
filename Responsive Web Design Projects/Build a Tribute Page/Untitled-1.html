About ⋅ Oskar Barnack

Oskar Barnack (1979-1936) was a German optical engineer, precision mechanic, industrial designer and father of 35mm photography. Very little is known about his personal life. Most of the information about his life revolves around his career at Ernst Leitz (now known as Leica Camera) where he designed the Ur-Leica, a prototype 35mm camera which was developed into the Leica range of viewfinder cameras and further developed into the Leica range of Leica Screw Mount rangefinders.

"Barnack’s ingenious invention set fourth new eras in photography, setting new standards for photographic equipment, amateur photographers, art photographers and photojournalists. Leica’s mystique was almost instantaneous; the Leica 35mm camera was the single most influential camera, and it was the most imitated."

– Lori Oden, International Photography Hall of Fame and Museum

A Timeline of Oskar Barnack's Career

  • 1879 - Born on November 1 in Lynow, Brandenburg, Germany.
  • 1893 - Begins an apprenticeship as a precision mechanic.
  • 1901 - Works as a precision mechanic in microscope development at Carl Zeiss.
  • 1911 - Joins Erntz Leitz as a master mechanic at the experimental workshop in the microscope department (now known as Leica Microsystems)
  • 1912 - Constructs a 35mm cine camera
  • 1913 - Becomes Head of Development, and develops the Ur-Leica, a prototype camera using 35mm motion picture film
  • 1914 - The First World War stops the development of Leica's 35mm still picture camera. Barnack works on optical development for military use.
  • 1920 - Development of the 35mm still picture camera begins.
  • 1925 - The first Leica cameras were made commercially available.
  • 1936 - Oskar Barnack dies on January 16 in Bad Nauheim, Germany.
  • 1979 - On the 100th anniversary of Barnack's birth, World Press Photo instituted the Oskar Barnack Award, which is administered by Leica Camera.
  • 1984 - Oscar Barnack is inducted into the International Photography Hall of Fame and Museum.
